If you're staying at the White Hart Hotel, Bude is the obvious first-day destination. It's just 17 miles west — less than half an hour by car — and it offers everything you'd want from a North Cornwall coast town: a long sandy beach, good surf, a charming town centre, an extraordinary tidal swimming pool, and enough good places to eat that you'll struggle to choose.

It's also far enough from the tourist honeypots of Padstow and Newquay that it hasn't been ruined. Bude is busy in summer, but busy in a friendly, family way — not crowded-out and overpriced in the way some Cornish towns have become.

Here's everything you need for a brilliant day.

## The Beach

Bude has two main beaches within walking distance of the town centre:

### Summerleaze Beach
The town beach — broad, sandy, backed by the dunes and the sea wall. This is where most people go because it's the easiest to reach and has the best facilities (toilets, lifeguards in season, café). The surf here is gentler than Crooklets, which makes it better for families with younger children.

The extraordinary feature of Summerleaze is the **tidal swimming pool** — a large seawater pool cut into the rocks at the southern end of the beach. It fills and empties with the tide, which means the water is always clean and always cold. On a hot August day it's absolutely packed; on a grey October morning you might have it entirely to yourself. One of the best outdoor swimming spots in England.

### Crooklets Beach
A five-minute walk north from Summerleaze. Bigger surf, more serious waves, more surfers. Lifeguarded in season. Better for those who want to get in the water properly rather than paddle at the edge.

## Surfing

Bude is a genuine surf town — not a pretend one. The waves here are real Atlantic swell, and there are several decent surf schools operating from Crooklets and Summerleaze if you want to try it or improve.

**Recommended for beginners:** Book a lesson rather than hiring a board and winging it. The currents can be strong and the rip tides are real. An hour with an instructor will teach you more and keep you safer.

## The Town Centre

Bude's town centre is small but genuinely good — compact enough to walk in half an hour but with enough independent shops that you'll want to spend longer.

**The Canal:** Bude has the only surviving stretch of a canal that once served the whole region. A short walk east of the town centre, it's beautiful, peaceful, and comes as a complete surprise to first-time visitors — a narrow canal through water meadows, completely at odds with the wild coast half a mile away. Good for a post-lunch stroll.

**Bude Castle:** Not actually a castle — a Regency villa built in 1830 now used as a heritage centre. Free to enter. Good for a rainy hour and genuinely interesting if you want to understand the history of the area.

## Things to Do Beyond the Beach

### Bude Sea Pool (Free)
Already mentioned above but worth repeating — the tidal seawater pool at Summerleaze is one of the best free things in Cornwall. Don't miss it.

### South West Coast Path
The path runs along both sides of Bude. Heading north from Crooklets takes you toward [Sandymouth] and beyond; heading south from Summerleaze takes you toward Widemouth Bay (3 miles). Both directions offer spectacular cliff walking. Take water, wear layers, and check the weather before you go.

### Widemouth Bay (3 miles south)
If Bude is too busy — and it can be in August — drive or walk 3 miles south to Widemouth Bay. Longer beach, fewer people, equally good surf, better parking. Often our first recommendation to guests who ask about beaches.

### The Canal Walk (2 miles each way)
Walk east from Bude along the canal to the first lock and back. Completely flat, beautiful, and takes about 90 minutes at a comfortable pace. Good for families, dogs, and anyone who wants to move without effort.

### Efford Down and the Headland
Walk to the headland north of Summerleaze for views back over Bude Bay. Twenty minutes from the town centre. Worth it for the perspective — you understand why the town is where it is.

## When to Go

**Best:** Late May, June, September. Weather is usually good, it's not peak-season busy, and the coast path is at its finest.

**Good:** July and August if you accept the crowds. Go early, stay late. Avoid arriving between 10am and 2pm in school holidays if you want to park.

**Underrated:** October. The surf gets better as the Atlantic storms arrive, the beaches are empty, the light is extraordinary, and the cafés are still open. If you're a walker or a surfer, October is the secret best month.

**Avoid:** The two weeks of the school summer holiday peak unless you enjoy standing in car park queues.

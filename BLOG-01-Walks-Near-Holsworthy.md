Holsworthy sits at the centre of one of the most underwalked corners of England. Not because there's nothing to walk — quite the opposite. The countryside around here is genuinely spectacular: rolling Devon farmland, the deep-cut valleys of the Rivers Tamar and Torridge, the open expanse of Dartmoor to the east, and the dramatic coastal paths of North Cornwall just 20 minutes west.

The reason people don't know about it is the same reason we love it: it hasn't been discovered yet. The car parks aren't full. The paths aren't worn to mud. You can walk for three hours on a Sunday in August and not see another soul.

Here are ten walks we'd recommend to guests staying at the White Hart Hotel — from a gentle hour-long stroll to a proper full-day moorland epic.

---

## 1. Holsworthy Town Loop (2 miles | Easy | 45 minutes)

**Start/finish:** White Hart Hotel, town centre

The simplest introduction to the area — and genuinely lovely if you arrive on a Tuesday, when Holsworthy's pannier market fills the square with local produce, crafts, and the kind of atmosphere you only find in working market towns.

The loop takes you out through the churchyard of St Peter and St Paul (one of Devon's finest churches — worth at least ten minutes inside), down along the edge of the Deer Park, and back through the old market area. It's a warm-up walk, a leg-stretcher after a long drive, or something to do before Sunday lunch.

**What to look for:** The church roof, the view south from Deer Park Lane, the original market buildings.

---

## 2. Tamar Valley Short Walk — Boyton (4 miles | Easy-Moderate | 2 hours)

**Start:** Boyton village, 8 miles from Holsworthy (park in the village)

The River Tamar marks the Devon-Cornwall border and runs south from near Holsworthy to Plymouth. This section is green, quiet, and barely visited — the path follows the river through ancient woodland and open fields before looping back through farmland.

It's a walk that rewards the slower approach: good bird life (kingfishers if you're lucky), otter spraint on the rocks if you know what you're looking for, and the particular silence of a river valley in the morning.

**Terrain:** Some mud after rain. Stiles. Not suitable for pushchairs.
**Pub at the end:** None on route — the White Hart is 8 miles back.

---

## 3. Dunsland Cross Circular (5 miles | Easy-Moderate | 2.5 hours)

**Start:** Dunsland Cross, approx 5 miles east of Holsworthy

Gently rolling farmland with long views south and east toward the edge of Dartmoor. This circular route follows quiet lanes, green lanes, and footpaths through a landscape that feels genuinely unchanged from the 1950s. Very few walkers. Very good for the mind.

Good on any day of the week but particularly fine on a clear autumn morning when the light comes in low from the east and everything looks like a Rowland Hilder painting.

**Terrain:** Easy. Some lane walking. Good boots recommended after rain.

---

## 4. Roadford Lake — Cycle/Walk Route (4-8 miles | Easy | 1.5-3 hours depending on distance)

**Start:** Roadford Lake, approx 8 miles southeast of Holsworthy

Roadford is Devon's largest inland lake — a reservoir created in the 1980s that now looks as though it's been there forever. The path around the lake is easy, traffic-free, and completely family-friendly, which makes it a rarity in this part of Devon.

The full circuit is about 8 miles. Most families do the southern section to the hide and back — 4 miles, 90 minutes, brilliant for children who want to spot the ducks and grebes. The birdwatching is genuinely good: great crested grebes breed here, goosanders in winter, and a regular osprey sighting most years during migration.

**Facilities:** Car park, toilets, café (check opening times)
**Good for:** Families, dogs, cyclists on easy bikes, older walkers

## 5. Tarka Trail — Meeth Section (6 miles | Easy | 2.5 hours)

**Start:** Meeth, approx 12 miles southeast of Holsworthy

The Tarka Trail follows the fictional journey of Tarka the Otter through North Devon. The surfaced section from Meeth to Petrockstow (and beyond to Torrington and Bideford) is one of Devon's great cycling routes — but it's also a walking route if you'd rather do it on foot.

This section follows the disused Torrington-Halwill railway line through deep woodland cuttings and over long valley viaducts. It's flat, car-free, and would be peaceful if the puffin-painted cycling families weren't also using it. Worth it for the viaducts.

**Getting there:** Drive to Meeth, park at the layby, walk west
**Note:** The surfaced trail is also excellent for road bikes and e-bikes

## 6. Okehampton and Dartmoor Edge (6-10 miles | Moderate-Challenging | 3-5 hours)

**Start:** Okehampton, 15 miles east of Holsworthy

This is where walks start getting serious. Okehampton sits on the northern edge of Dartmoor and has excellent direct access to the high moorland via the Tarka Trail on its eastern edge or the footpath network heading south.

The most accessible good walk heads south from the town past the ruins of Okehampton Castle (worth a stop — atmospheric motte and bailey ruin on a wooded gorge) and up onto the open moor above. Clear days offer views to Yes Tor and High Willhays; on bad days, the clouds come in fast and you understand why maps and compasses exist.

**What to bring:** Map, compass, waterproofs, proper footwear. Dartmoor is not forgiving to the unprepared.
**Extend it:** Continue to Belstone (6 miles from Okehampton) for a loop over the high moor

## 7. Belstone and Dartmoor (8 miles | Challenging | 4 hours)

**Start:** Belstone village, 16 miles from Holsworthy

Belstone is one of the most beautifully situated villages on Dartmoor. Park in the village (free, but limited) and head straight onto the open moor — within ten minutes of the village green you're on open grassland with views that go on forever on a clear day.

The classic Belstone loop takes in the Nine Stones (ancient stone circle, probably Bronze Age), the ridge above Belstone Common, and the river valleys below. It's the walk that makes you understand why people move to Devon.

**Warning:** Navigation required in poor visibility. Download the OS map offline before you go. The moor does not care that you have a smartphone with no signal.
**Best season:** Autumn (bracken turning, fewer tourists) or winter (snow on the tops, extraordinary light)

---

## 8. Lydford Gorge (2 miles | Easy-Moderate | 1.5 hours)

**Start:** National Trust car park, Lydford, 14 miles from Holsworthy

Lydford Gorge is one of Devon's most dramatic landscapes — a deep, steep-sided gorge carved by the River Lyd, with a series of waterfalls including the 30-metre White Lady Fall at the southern end. The National Trust manages the path, which follows the gorge from top to bottom and back.

It's accessible, well-maintained, and the gorge itself is genuinely vertiginous in places — the path narrows to single file above the river in several spots. Not suitable for young children without close supervision. Dogs allowed.

**Facilities:** NT car park, café, toilets
**Cost:** National Trust admission (free for NT members)

---

## 9. Bude Canal and Coast Path (4-8 miles | Easy-Moderate | 2-4 hours)

**Start:** Bude town centre, 17 miles from Holsworthy

Bude's canal is the only surviving bit of a longer canal that once served the whole area — it's a lovely, flat walk east from the town through meadows and over aqueducts, completely at odds with the wild coast visible to the west. Good for families, pushchairs, and anyone who wants to feel smug about walking while looking at a place where other people are surfing.

The coast path alternative heads south from Bude to Widemouth Bay — 3 miles with good views and a proper beach at the end. Harder than the canal walk but more dramatic.

**Combine both:** Bude canal south, coast path north — circular via Bude town, approx 7 miles.

---

## 10. Crackington Haven and the Coast Path (5 miles | Challenging | 3 hours)

**Start:** Crackington Haven, 23 miles from Holsworthy

Save this one for a good day. Crackington Haven is a tiny cove at the bottom of a dramatic valley on the North Cornwall coast — and the South West Coast Path either side is among the most spectacular walking in England. The cliffs here are over 400 feet, the rock formations are extraordinary, and the views on a clear day stretch as far as Hartland Point to the north and Tintagel to the south.

Head north from the Haven to Cambeak and back (3 miles, easy to follow), or take the southern option over High Cliff (at 731 feet, Cornwall's highest cliff) toward St Gennys.

**Warning:** Exposed coastal path. Wind can be fierce. Good footwear essential — the paths are not always well-maintained.
**What to bring:** Waterproofs even on good days — coastal weather is unpredictable.

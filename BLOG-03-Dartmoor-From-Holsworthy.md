Dartmoor is thirty minutes from the White Hart Hotel. That's probably not what most people expect when they think about Holsworthy — the town is in the north, near Cornwall, far from what most visitors think of as "Devon" — but it's true. Drive east and within half an hour you're standing on open moorland with views that stretch for fifteen miles.

This is one of the great hidden advantages of staying in Holsworthy. You can do the coast in one direction and the moor in the other, on the same weekend, without a long drive either way.

This guide covers everything you need to make the most of a day on Dartmoor from Holsworthy.

## What Dartmoor Actually Is

Dartmoor National Park covers 368 square miles of upland Devon — the largest and wildest stretch of open country in southern England. Its highest point is High Willhays at 621 metres (2,037 feet), on the northern moor above Okehampton. The landscape is ancient: Bronze Age standing stones, stone rows, and cairns are scattered across the moor in numbers that will surprise you. Wild ponies graze freely. Red grouse call from the heather. Ravens circle the tors.

It's also a working landscape — Dartmoor farmers have grazed cattle and sheep on the commons for centuries, which is part of why it looks the way it does. The landscape is actively maintained by that grazing. Take nothing, leave nothing, stay on the rights of way where they exist.

## Getting to Dartmoor from Holsworthy

**Okehampton** is the gateway town — 15 miles east of Holsworthy, about 25 minutes by car via the A3072 and A30. From Okehampton you can access the northern moor directly.

**Belstone** is a small village 3 miles southeast of Okehampton. Most serious walkers drive to Belstone and start their moorland walk from there — it gets you onto the open moor faster.

**The A30** runs along the northern edge of the moor. From Holsworthy, take the A3072 to Okehampton and you'll pick up the A30 there.

**Other access points from Holsworthy:**
- Lydford: 14 miles · ~22 minutes (via A30 junction)
- Tavistock: 23 miles · ~40 minutes (south Dartmoor edge)

## The Best Walks (for different abilities)

### The Easy Option: Okehampton Castle (1.5 miles | 1 hour)

Not strictly on the moor but worth the visit. Okehampton Castle is an atmospheric ruin in a wooded gorge just south of the town — one of Devon's largest medieval castles and genuinely impressive. English Heritage manage it.

The walk from the town centre is short and easy, with the castle visible from the valley floor as you approach. Good for families, anyone with limited mobility, or anyone who wants a taste of the area without committing to a full moorland day.

**English Heritage admission applies. Free for members.**

---

### The Family Walk: Belstone Common (3-4 miles | 2 hours | Easy-Moderate)

**Start:** Belstone village car park (free)

Park in Belstone village and take the track south onto the common. Within 15 minutes you're on open moorland with the tors visible above you.

The most popular route loops around Belstone Common — staying on the lower moorland, following the Taw River valley, and returning via the ridge. Good views, no serious navigation required on a clear day, and short enough that children and slower walkers will manage without complaint.

**The Nine Stones:** About 1.5 miles from Belstone, easily reached on this route — a Bronze Age stone circle with nine stones (there may originally have been more). Less famous than the larger circles in the south, but quieter and more atmospheric for it.

**What to bring:** Waterproofs (the weather changes quickly), good footwear, water. The bog is real — the spongy ground can be ankle-deep after rain even on what look like innocuous paths.

---

### The Full Day: Yes Tor and High Willhays (8-10 miles | 5-6 hours | Challenging)

**Start:** Okehampton Camp (MOD training area — access varies, check current restrictions)
**Alternative start:** Meldon Reservoir car park (paid), 3 miles west of Okehampton

This is the serious Dartmoor walk from this area — up to the highest ground in southern England. Yes Tor (619m) and High Willhays (621m) are the two summits, close together, with extraordinary views on clear days to the coast in both directions.

The walk up from Meldon Reservoir takes about 2.5 hours to reach the summits. The return by a different route adds variety and avoids retracing steps. Total distance varies by the exact route taken — 8-10 miles is typical.

**IMPORTANT:** Dartmoor's high moor is serious upland terrain. Navigation by map and compass is required in poor visibility — phone signal is unreliable. Weather can change within minutes. Go in good weather, go prepared, and tell someone where you're going.

**Military training area note:** Part of the high moor around Okehampton is a live military training area. Red flags are flown when firing is in progress. Check current access at gov.uk/guidance/dartmoor-training-area before visiting.

---

### The Hidden Gem: Sourton Tors and Corn Ridge (6 miles | 3.5 hours | Moderate)

**Start:** Sourton, just off the A386 south of Okehampton

Fewer walkers than the Belstone route, equally beautiful, and with the bonus of Sourton Tors — a dramatic rocky outcrop with views across to Yes Tor and the high moor. The ridge walk from here to Corn Ridge is one of the finest short ridge walks on the northern moor.

The return via the valley is gentler and follows a lovely stream — good for children at the end of a long day when legs are getting tired.

## Lydford — Worth a Detour

Lydford is 14 miles from Holsworthy — a short detour off the A30 that's worth making.

**Lydford Gorge (National Trust):** The most dramatic piece of landscape within easy reach of Holsworthy. The River Lyd has carved a deep, narrow gorge through the rock — the path follows it from top to bottom, with the 30-metre White Lady Waterfall at the southern end. Some sections are genuinely vertiginous, with the path at head height above the river.

Allow 2-3 hours for the full circuit. NT admission applies. Good café at the entrance.

**Lydford Castle:** A short walk from the NT car park — a small Norman castle that was later used as a prison and stannary court. Free to visit (English Heritage). More atmospheric than large.

**The Dartmoor Inn at Lydford:** A good place for lunch after the gorge — well-regarded gastropub with reliably good food.

## Princetown and the Southern Moor (45-50 minutes from Holsworthy)

If you want to go further and see the classic "movie Dartmoor" landscape — open, bleak, brooding, with Dartmoor Prison visible in the distance — Princetown is the destination. It sits at the heart of the high southern moor and is reached via Tavistock or via the B3212 from Moretonhampstead.

**The High Moorland Visitor Centre** in Princetown is an excellent starting point and is free to enter — good exhibits on the landscape, history, and wildlife, plus maps and advice.

**From Princetown:** The walk to Tor Royal is easy and gives you the moorland experience quickly. For something more challenging, the route via South Hessary Tor and Nun's Cross Farm is a brilliant moorland circuit.

**Honest note about distance:** Princetown is about 40-45 minutes from Holsworthy. It's worth the drive if you want the high southern moor, but the northern moor via Okehampton/Belstone is quicker from our door.

## Wildlife to Watch For

Dartmoor supports some excellent wildlife — more than most walkers realise:

**Dartmoor ponies:** Free-roaming, genuine wild ponies — technically they're owned by local farmers but they run free on the moor. Don't feed them. Enjoy them from a respectful distance. They are genuinely wonderful.

**Red grouse:** Common on the heather moorland in late summer. You'll hear them before you see them — a distinctive "go-back, go-back" call. Fly low and fast.

**Ravens:** Large, black, intelligent. The "cronk" call is distinctive. Often seen circling the tors.

**Buzzards:** Everywhere. You'll see them on almost any moorland day.

**Merlin (rare):** The UK's smallest falcon. A possibility on open heather moorland if you're lucky.

**Dartmoor's bogs:** The blanket bog supports rare plants including sundew (a carnivorous plant that catches insects) and various specialist mosses. Look down as well as up.

## What to Bring

This isn't scare-mongering — Dartmoor genuinely requires more preparation than most English countryside:

✓ **Waterproofs** (jacket and trousers) — even if it looks fine when you leave
✓ **Proper walking boots** — trail shoes work in summer, but boots are safer for the boggy ground
✓ **OS map** (OL28: Dartmoor 1:25,000) — download offline on the OS Maps app **before** you go (no signal on the high moor)
✓ **Compass** — and know how to use it, at least at a basic level
✓ **Water and food** — no shops or cafés on the open moor
✓ **Fully charged phone** — for emergency use, not navigation
✓ **Layers** — it can be 10°C colder on the high moor than in Holsworthy
